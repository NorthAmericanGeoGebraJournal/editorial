# Conflicts of interest

> **DRAFT — for the editorial board to revise.** Placeholder wording, adapted
> from common practice at open-review journals. Nothing here is in force yet.

A conflict is not misconduct. It is a fact to be declared, so that other people
can judge it. Declaring one costs nothing; concealing one costs the journal its
credibility.

## When you have one

*(Board to confirm each of these, including the time windows.)*

You likely have a conflict with an author if you:

- are at the **same institution**, now or within the last *(N)* years
- have **co-authored** with them within the last *(N)* years
- have a **current or recent collaboration**, grant, or shared project
- are, or were, their **student, supervisor, or mentor**
- have a **personal relationship** — family, close friendship, or the opposite
- have a **financial interest** in the outcome

## If you are a reviewer

Say so in the issue, before you start. The editor will find someone else. You
lose nothing by declaring one.

If you are unsure, declare it anyway and let the editor decide. That is the
whole point of open review: the reader can see who reviewed what, so a
borderline call that was declared is fine, and one that was hidden is not.

## If you are an editor

Hand the submission to another editor and say so in the thread. Do not handle a
paper you have a conflict with, even if you are confident you could be fair —
the reader cannot see your confidence, only the record.

## If you are an author

Declare, in the submission issue, anyone who should not review your paper, and
why. "None" is a fine answer.

## A small journal problem

NAGJ's field is small, and its likely reviewers are people who know each other.
A rule strict enough to eliminate every connection would leave nobody able to
review anything.

So the standard is **disclosure, not purity**: say what the connection is, let
the editor decide, and let the public record show both. *(Board: this is the
part most worth arguing about.)*
