# Reviewer checklist

> **DRAFT — for the editorial board to revise.** Placeholder wording. The items
> below are a starting point, not policy.

Copy this into the review issue and work through it in the open. Tick what
passes, comment on what doesn't. **Partial reviews are useful** — post as you go
rather than saving it all for the end.

You are reviewing a **paper about teaching mathematics with GeoGebra**, not a
piece of software. Judge it as scholarship.

## Conflicts

- [ ] I have no conflict of interest with the authors (see
      [the policy](../policies/conflicts-of-interest.md))
- [ ] I am willing to have my name on this review permanently and publicly

If either is a problem, say so now and stop. Neither reflects badly on you.

## Scope

- [ ] The work concerns GeoGebra in mathematics education, K–16
- [ ] It would interest NAGJ's readers — teachers and mathematics educators

## Mathematics

- [ ] The mathematics is **correct**
- [ ] Claims are justified; proofs, where given, are sound
- [ ] Notation and terminology are standard, or defined
- [ ] Any errors found are noted below, with specifics

> Rigour appropriate to the audience. A classroom activity is not held to a
> research paper's standard of generality — but it must not be *wrong*.

## Pedagogy

- [ ] The educational purpose is clear: what should a learner come away with?
- [ ] The approach is plausible for the grade level claimed
- [ ] GeoGebra does real work here — it is not decoration on a static idea
- [ ] Claims about student learning are supported, or presented as the authors'
      experience rather than evidence

## Reproducibility

**The central question: could a reader recreate this?**

- [ ] A competent reader could rebuild the construction from the paper alone
- [ ] The route is clear — construction commands, a described procedure,
      figures a reader can follow, or a link to a working resource
- [ ] Figures are legible and actually show what the text says
- [ ] Nothing essential exists only in the authors' heads

> Any of those routes is acceptable. What is not acceptable is a paper that
> cannot be followed by any of them.

## GeoGebra resource *(if one exists)*

**Not required.** A paper with no applet is not deficient — skip this section and
say so. If the authors did provide one:

- [ ] The link resolves
- [ ] It loads and works
- [ ] It does what the paper says it does
- [ ] It is hosted somewhere permanent — normally geogebra.org
- [ ] It is usable: labelled, sensible defaults, not broken by ordinary dragging

If a resource is broken or missing, that is a **fixable revision**, not grounds
for rejection — unless the paper depends on it to be followable at all.

- [ ] If there is no resource: the paper stands without one

## Presentation

- [ ] Structure and language are clear enough to follow
- [ ] References are complete, and sources of ideas are credited
- [ ] Figures and tables are captioned and referred to in the text
- [ ] Any prior work this builds on is acknowledged

## Recommendation

Choose one and say why:

- [ ] **Accept** — publishable as is
- [ ] **Minor revision** — fixable without another review round
- [ ] **Major revision** — needs substantive work and a second look
- [ ] **Reject** — out of scope, or not repairable through revision

**Comments to the author:**

_Be specific and be kind. Name what works before what doesn't. Every comment is
public and permanent, and the author is a colleague._

## Notes for reviewers

**Review the paper in front of you**, not the one you would have written.

**Distinguish "wrong" from "not how I'd do it."** Both are worth saying; only the
first blocks publication.

**Say what's good.** Public review makes praise permanent too, and it is the
cheapest thing you can give a colleague.

**Take the time you need.** If you're stuck, say so in the issue — a stalled
review helps nobody, and the editor would rather know.
